ARRIVAL · CANADA

Day 1 in Canada: One of Us Was Ready. One of Us Really Wasn't.

By StudenzBit Team · 8 min read · Arrival · Canada

Arjun

Mumbai → Toronto

"I'll figure it out when I get there."

Priya

Delhi → Toronto

"I did my research. With StudenzBit."

Two characters. Same flight. Same destination. Completely different first days.

THE SETUP

Flight AC044 from Delhi touches down at Toronto Pearson International Airport at 6:47am.

Both Arjun and Priya are on it.

Same turbulence. Same bad airline food. Same exhaustion after 14 hours in the air. Same dream — a degree, a career, a life in Canada.

But what happens in the next four hours? That's where the stories split.

Arjun's Story

Mumbai → Toronto

The moment the wheels hit the runway, Arjun reaches for his phone. He needs to tell his mum he landed. She's been up all night.

No signal.

He fumbles through his bag, switches on international roaming — nothing. He spots a 'Pearson Guest WiFi' network but his phone won't connect. The line is already moving. His mum doesn't know he's landed.

It's fine, he tells himself. I'll text her once I'm through.

He joins the immigration queue with hundreds of other international students. The officer at the desk is serious, efficient. She asks for three things: his Passport, his Letter of Acceptance (LOA) from his university, and his Letter of Introduction (LOI) from IRCC.

Arjun nods confidently. He has all of them.

On Google Drive.

His face changes. He searches for airport WiFi again — this time he finds a signal, barely. Two bars. He opens Drive. The loading circle spins. And spins. The officer waits. The people behind Arjun wait. His palms are sweating more than they did in his visa interview.

Finally — it loads. He turns the screen toward the officer. She looks at it. Nods. Stamps his passport.

Arjun exhales. Crisis averted. He tucks his new study permit straight into his bag without checking it — relieved just to be through.

What he doesn't know is that the expiry date on that permit matches the exact month his final exams end. A detail that will cost him a lot of stress two years from now.

(More on that in the tip below.)

Next comes customs. Arjun hasn't heard of the ArriveCAN app. He joins the long declaration queue, fills out a paper form while half-asleep, and shuffles forward for 25 minutes.

He spots a Service Canada kiosk on the way out — he knows he'll need a SIN number at some point — but the line snakes around the corner and Arjun barely has the energy to stand. He walks past it. Good call, actually.

He collects his two overstuffed suitcases and wheels them into the arrivals hall. He's waiting for his friend who said he'd be there.

That's when she appears.

A woman at a carrier kiosk — warm smile, red jacket, laminated deal sheet in hand. 'Welcome to Canada! First month free, just sign here.' She sees the study permit in Arjun's hand before he can put it away. She's clearly done this before.

The deal sounds good. First month free. No payment needed today. Arjun hands over his passport and study permit. Five minutes later he has a new SIM card and a postpaid plan he doesn't fully understand.

Nobody tells him that 'first month free' is not the same as 'free.' The plan doesn't auto-cancel. When month two arrives, billing starts — silently. Students forget. Three months later the calls come. Bills piling up. Credit score affected.

Arjun doesn't know any of this yet. He slips the SIM card into his phone, satisfied.

His friend finally arrives — late, because he assumed immigration would take longer.

They load the bags into the car. The Terminal 1 doors slide open automatically. A wall of cold air hits Arjun in the face like a bucket of ice water.

It's December. It's -6°C. He's wearing a hoodie under a thin jacket he bought in Mumbai. He shivers the entire time they load the bags.

Arjun arrives at his residence at 11:30am. Four hours and forty-seven minutes from landing.

He sits on the floor of his empty room, still cold, completely drained, and stares at the ceiling.

'Why did nobody tell me any of this? At least I made it...'

He goes to sleep.

⚠️ What Arjun Should Have Done

  • Print your documents — LOA, LOI, Confirmation of Enrollment, proof of funds. One printed folder. Black and white is fine. Don't rely on cloud storage at a foreign airport on two bars of WiFi.
  • Check your study permit expiry date before you walk away from the immigration counter. The officer can extend it right there if you ask politely.
  • Complete ArriveCAN before you fly — 10 minutes on your phone the night before saves you a 25-minute paper queue on arrival.
  • Understand that "first month free" is not free — postpaid plans continue billing unless you call and cancel. Set a calendar reminder for Day 25.
  • Pack a winter jacket, gloves, and beanie in your carry-on — not your checked bag. Canada in December is not cold. It's a different category of cold.
  • Pre-book your airport pickup — if someone is collecting you, tell them immigration takes 45–90 minutes. Coordinate properly.

💡 Study Permit Expiry — Don't Skip This

If your program ends in December, your Letter of Completion and official transcripts won't arrive until January — universities close for Christmas. If your study permit expired in December, you cannot apply for the PGWP. You'd need to first renew your permit, adding weeks of delay and stress.

At the immigration counter, ask the officer to set your study permit expiry 90 days after your program end date. Most will do it without hesitation if you explain why.

To apply for PGWP you need all four at the same time: Active Study Permit + Letter of Completion + Official Transcripts + Language Test Scores.

💡 Skip Service Canada at the Airport

The Service Canada desk at Pearson helps you get your SIN number on arrival. After a 14-hour flight and 90-minute immigration process, that queue is the last place you want to be. Rest first. Get your SIN on Day 2 when you're rested and have your documents properly organised.

Priya's Story

Delhi → Toronto

At 6:47am, before Priya even clears the jetway, her phone buzzes. Data connected.

She set up a free SIM through VFS Global before leaving Delhi. It activated the moment Air Canada Flight AC044 touched Canadian soil. Two seconds later her mum has a WhatsApp message: 'Landed safely. All good. Love you.'

She joins the immigration queue with the same crowd as Arjun. Same officer. Same questions. But Priya's folder is already open on the desk before the officer finishes her sentence — passport on top, Letter of Acceptance second, Letter of Introduction third, Confirmation of Enrollment fourth, proof of funds fifth. Clean, tabbed, printed.

The officer asks four questions. Priya answers four questions. She's through in nine minutes.

Before leaving the counter, she glances at her study permit expiry date. She checked the StudenzBit arrival guide the night before and knew to look. The date is fine — 90 days after her program ends.

She walks to customs. She pulls out her phone, taps three buttons, and walks to the ArriveCAN kiosk. Scans her passport. Done in three minutes. No queue. No paper form.

At baggage claim, she opens the Uber app — already set up with a prepaid foreign exchange card she loaded before leaving India. $68 to her residence. She'd budgeted for it. She books the ride.

While she waits for her suitcase, she reaches into her carry-on and pulls out her winter jacket. Then her gloves. Then her beanie.

She's wearing all three by the time the Uber arrives.

The same carrier saleswoman approaches her at the arrivals hall with the laminated deal sheet.

'No thank you,' says Priya, without breaking stride. 'I'm sorting my SIM next week.'

She climbs into the Uber at 8:04am.

She arrives at her residence at 8:22am. Same chaotic airport. Same freezing December morning. Same city.

Completely different experience.

She unpacks her essentials, checks the first item off her StudenzBit arrival checklist, pulls up the Day 2 task list, and closes her eyes.

Ready for what comes next.

💡 Priya's Tip

The $68 Uber feels like a lot on Day 1. It is. But you've just flown 14 hours, you have two suitcases, and you've never taken transit in this city before. This is not the day to figure out the UP Express.

Spend the $68. Arrive calmly. Learn the transit system on Day 3 when you're rested and not carrying everything you own.

Also — complete your ArriveCAN declaration 72 hours before your flight. Not at the airport. Not on the plane. At home, the night before, on proper WiFi.

The Lesson

Arjun isn't stupid. He studied hard for four years to get here. He passed every English test, survived the visa process, and got on that plane. He just didn't know what he didn't know.

That's the thing about arriving in a new country — nobody gives you the manual. Nobody tells you that Google Drive fails on two bars of airport WiFi. Nobody tells you that 'first month free' doesn't mean 'auto-cancelled.' Nobody tells you that December in Toronto is a different species of cold.

Priya didn't get lucky. She got prepared.

Here's what to take away from Day 1:

  • Sort a temporary SIM before you land — check if your visa office (VFS Global etc.) provides a free one
  • Print your LOA, LOI, and Confirmation of Enrollment — never rely on cloud storage at a foreign airport
  • Check your study permit expiry date before leaving the counter — ask for 90 days past your program end date
  • Complete ArriveCAN 72 hours before your flight — skip the declaration queue entirely
  • Pack your winter jacket, gloves, and beanie in your carry-on — not your checked bag
  • Pre-book your airport transfer and coordinate pickup times properly
  • Skip Service Canada at the airport — get your SIN on Day 2 when you're rested
  • If anyone offers you a "free" postpaid phone plan at the airport — read every word before signing

What Happens Next?

Both Arjun and Priya need to open a bank account within their first three days.

Arjun will walk in twice before he figures out what documents to bring. The second time, he'll wait 45 minutes only to find out he's missing one thing.

Priya already knows exactly which bank, which branch, and what to bring.
