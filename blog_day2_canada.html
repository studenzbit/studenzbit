✈️ The Setup

Day 2 in Canada.

Last night felt like survival. Today feels like the beginning of something real.

Both Arjun and Priya slept. Both woke up to the same grey Toronto morning, the same unfamiliar ceiling, the same quiet hum of a city that doesn't know their name yet.

Two tasks stand between them and Day 3.

Task 1 — The SIN Number. Nine digits. Issued by the Canadian government. Without it, no employer can legally pay you. Without it, the bank won't process your credit card application tomorrow. It sounds simple. It isn't.

Task 2 — The SIM Card. A proper one this time. Not a kiosk deal at 7am after a 14-hour flight. A plan you actually chose, with terms you actually read, from a carrier you actually researched.

Get both right today and Day 3 — the bank account, the credit card, the foundation of your financial life in this country — goes smoothly. Get them wrong, and you spend the rest of the week backtracking.

🧳 Chapter One

Arjun's Story

Mumbai → Toronto

"I'll do the SIN online — it's faster, right?"

Arjun's alarm goes off at 9am. He lies there for a moment, staring at the ceiling of his friend's spare room. His suitcases are still half-unpacked against the wall.

He gets up.

He opens his laptop and Googles: how to get SIN number Canada.

Service Canada appears at the top. He clicks through. He sees two options:

In-person — visit a Service Canada office.
Online — apply from home, receive your SIN by mail.

By mail, he thinks. That sounds slow.

He clicks the online option anyway, just to see. The page loads. He reads.

Receive your SIN letter within 10 to 20 business days.

Ten to twenty business days.

Arjun does the math. Two to four weeks. He leans back in his chair. He needs the SIN for the bank tomorrow. But maybe the mail is faster than they say. Maybe it'll be fine.

He convinces himself it will be fine.

He fills in the form carefully. Name. Date of birth. Canadian address. He reaches for his documents.

He uploads his study permit approval letter — the one IRCC emailed him before he left India. The official-looking one. The one he's been showing to everyone since he landed.

It is not his actual study permit.

His actual study permit is the physical paper the CBSA officer printed and handed to him at Pearson on Day 1. It is sitting in his document folder three feet away from him right now. He doesn't know the difference.

He hits submit.

He waits for the confirmation email.

One minute. Two minutes. Nothing.

He checks his inbox. Promotions. Spam — nothing there either. Five minutes pass. His leg starts bouncing. Did it go through? Did the site time out?

He goes back and submits the form again, more carefully this time.

The confirmation email arrives in seconds.

He exhales. Done.

What he doesn't know — and won't find out for weeks — is that the first email did send. It went straight to spam. Both applications are now in the system. Service Canada flags duplicates for manual review. His SIN will take longer to arrive. He'll keep checking the mailbox without understanding why.

And when he finally sits down at the bank tomorrow and the banker asks for his SIN number to process his credit card — the silence that follows will tell the whole story.

He closes the laptop. Satisfied. Unaware.

He gets dressed and heads out. He's been wanting to upgrade his phone for months. There's a Rogers store nearby. He walks in.

The rep is friendly. iPhone 17, 128GB. New number. $95 a month plus taxes on a 24-month plan. Around $107 a month after Ontario HST.

Somewhere in the back of his mind, something flickers — a memory of a SIM card at the airport. But the phone is right there on the counter. The rep is already running the activation.

The thought disappears.

He walks out thirty minutes later with a brand new iPhone 17 and zero recollection that there is a postpaid plan on his old phone billing him $85 a month. Two active plans. Around $192 a month. He has no idea.

He passes a café on the walk back. Wooden tables, warm light inside. He goes in.

He orders a coffee. Sits by the window. Watches Toronto move past outside — people in coats, living their lives in this city that is now, somehow, his city too.

He thinks about his mum. About how far away everything familiar feels.

The coffee is very good.

Some days you just need to sit with it for a bit.

He stays for forty minutes.

He gets home at 2pm. New phone set up. SIN submitted with the wrong document, filed twice. Two plans billing him simultaneously. Day 3 tomorrow.

He puts his feet up.

Day 2, he thinks. Done.

"Day 2. Done."
He doesn't know yet that it isn't.

— Arjun, 2pm, spare room, Toronto

⚠️ What Arjun Should Have Done

  • ✗Chose online SIN without understanding the delivery time — online means mail, and mail means 10–20 business days. If you need it before your bank appointment, go in person. You get it the same day.
  • ✗Uploaded the wrong document — the IRCC approval letter is NOT your study permit. Your study permit is the physical paper the CBSA officer handed you at the airport on Day 1. Always bring the airport paper.
  • ✗Submitted the SIN form twice — always check your spam folder before resubmitting anything on a government website. Duplicate submissions get flagged and slow processing.
  • ✗Bought a new phone before cancelling his airport SIM — postpaid plans do not stop billing because you stopped using them. He now has two active plans running simultaneously at ~$192/month total.
  • ✗Signed a 24-month contract before his first paycheque — Day 2 is too early to commit. The phone will still be there in Month 2. Sort your finances first.

💡 Online vs In-Person SIN — What You Actually Need to Know

Online (canada.ca):

→ SIN arrives by mail in 10–20 business days

→ Fine if you do not need it urgently

→ Most common mistake: uploading the approval letter instead of the actual study permit

In-Person (Service Canada office):

→ Book at servicecanada.gc.ca or walk in

→ Bring: passport + physical study permit (the airport paper — not the email)

→ SIN issued same day, usually in 15–20 minutes

The rule: If you need your SIN before your bank appointment — and you do — go in person on Day 2. Do not gamble on mail delivery.

💡 Three Documents. Three Different Things. Know Which One They're Asking For.

Study Permit Approval Letter (Port of Entry Letter of Introduction):
The PDF IRCC emailed you before you left your home country. You used it to board your flight and enter Canada.
It is NOT your study permit.

TRV Stamp in your passport:
The visa stamp IRCC placed in your passport in India. It allowed you to travel to Canada.
It is NOT your study permit.

Your Actual Study Permit:
The printed paper the CBSA officer handed you at Pearson Airport on Day 1. This is your study permit. This is what Service Canada, your bank, and every Canadian institution needs to see.

Always bring the airport paper. Always.

💡 Your SIN, Passport & Study Permit — Guard These With Your Life

Your Social Insurance Number is the most sensitive number you own in Canada. With it, someone can open credit in your name, file fraudulent tax returns, and commit identity fraud that takes years to undo. The same applies to your passport and study permit — in the wrong hands, they can be used to impersonate you or compromise your immigration status.

Follow every one of these rules:

  • Memorise your SIN. Do not store it in your phone notes, WhatsApp, or any app.
  • Never photograph your passport or study permit and send it over WhatsApp or Instagram — even to family.
  • Never share your SIN with friends, roommates, or anyone who is not an official institution.
  • Never fill in your SIN on a job application before you have a signed offer letter. Legitimate employers ask for it after hiring — not before.
  • Never submit personal documents on any website that is not an official government domain (.gc.ca) or a verified Canadian institution.
  • If anyone pressures you to share these details urgently — stop. Verify first. International student scams are common, well-organised, and convincing.

If in doubt: pause. Verify. Then proceed.

🌟 Chapter Two

Priya's Story

Delhi → Toronto

"Service Canada. 9:30am. Documents ready."

Priya's alarm goes off at 7:30am.

She'd booked the Service Canada appointment the same night she landed — from the back seat of the Uber, while her family's WhatsApp messages were still coming through. The city was dark outside the window, lights blurring past. She had her document folder open on her lap. It took four minutes. There was a 9:30am slot available the very next morning.

She showers. Layers properly — she checked the weather before bed. -2°C. She knew.

At 9:05am she taps her prepaid card at the TTC turnstile and boards the subway heading downtown. She arrives at the Service Canada office at 9:22am, checks in at the front desk, and takes a seat.

She's called at 9:33am.

"Passport and study permit please."

She opens her folder without hesitating. She hands over her passport and the physical paper the CBSA officer gave her at Pearson on Day 1. Not the approval letter. Not a screenshot. The actual airport document. She'd read about the difference in the StudenzBit arrival guide two nights ago, sitting in her room going through every item on the checklist.

The officer looks at both documents. Types quietly. The room is calm — filing cabinets, fluorescent lights, the soft sound of keyboards.

The printer hums.

"Here's your SIN."

Priya takes the paper. She reads the nine digits once, quietly to herself. Then again. She checks the expiry date — it matches her study permit end date exactly, the way it should for international students on temporary permits.

She tucks it into her document folder.

She does not type it into her phone. She memorises it. Treats it like a PIN. Never to be stored digitally. Never to be shared casually. She'd read that too.

It is 9:51am.

She walks out into the cold, pulls her jacket tighter, and allows herself one quiet moment of satisfaction.

She passes the café she'd saved on Google Maps two days ago — small place, good reviews, wooden tables visible through the window. She goes in.

Flat white. Pain au chocolat. Table by the window.

She opens her notebook and goes through the Day 3 checklist while she drinks her coffee.

Bank appointment: 10am tomorrow. Scotiabank on Bloor. Documents: passport, study permit, university acceptance letter, SIN number. ✓ All ready.

She adds one more line at the bottom: ask about student credit card at same appointment. No annual fee. Forever.

She stays for 35 minutes. She's earned it.

After the café, she heads to a Fido store. She'd decided on the plan the night before — around $35–45/month depending on the current deal, Rogers network, no contract. She'd also decided to keep her old phone for now. It works fine. She'll upgrade when she's actually earning and the right deal comes along.

The store rep sets up the new SIM in 18 minutes. She walks out with a new Canadian number and texts her family to save it.

She's back at her residence by 12:30pm.

SIN: done.
SIM: done.
Coffee: had.
Day 3: ready.

She unpacks the last of her bags, puts on a podcast, and feels — for the first time since landing — like she actually lives here.

"SIN done. SIM done. Coffee had. Day 3 — ready."

— Priya, 12:30pm, residence, Toronto

✅ Priya's Tips

Book your Service Canada appointment the same night you land — from the Uber, from your bed, wherever. Slots in downtown Toronto fill up fast, especially Monday and Tuesday mornings. If you wait until Day 2 morning to book, you might end up with a Day 4 or 5 slot — which pushes your bank account opening back by days.

You can also walk in without an appointment. There will usually be a wait of around 30–45 minutes, but it works. Do not let the booking step stop you from going.

Check your SIN expiry date before you leave the Service Canada office. As an international student on a temporary permit, your SIN starts with the digit 9 and expires on the same date as your study permit. When you renew your study permit, you must also renew your SIN. Keep the paper in your document folder — you will need it again.

Keep your old phone for now. The money you don't spend on a new handset in Week 1 is money you have for rent, groceries, transit, and the actual cost of starting over in a new country. Upgrade when you're earning. The phone doesn't care.

And take yourself out for a coffee somewhere between errands. You've done something genuinely hard. A 35-minute break with something good to drink is not procrastination — it's maintenance.

The Lesson

Arjun didn't fail because he was lazy. He made three assumptions that felt completely reasonable in the moment:

Online must be faster than in-person.
The document IRCC sent me must be the right one.
That airport SIM thing will sort itself out.

None of them were true.

The gap between him and Priya isn't effort. It's information. She knew which document to bring. She knew online means mail delivery. She knew that a new phone plan requires cancelling the old one first. And she still found time for a flat white.

Here's what to take away from Day 2:

  • ✓If you need your SIN before your bank appointment — go in person. Online takes 10–20 business days.
  • ✓Your study permit is the paper you got from the airport — not the approval letter IRCC emailed you, not the stamp in your passport.
  • ✓Book Service Canada the same night you land — do not wait until Day 2 morning. Slots go fast.
  • ✓Check your SIN expiry date before leaving the office — it should match your study permit end date.
  • ✓Memorise your SIN — do not store it in your phone, notes app, or WhatsApp. Treat it like a PIN.
  • ✓Never share your SIN, passport, or study permit with anyone who is not an official institution — not friends, not job forms before an offer, nobody.
  • ✓If you are getting a new phone plan, cancel the old one the same day — postpaid plans do not stop billing because you stopped using them.
  • ✓Keep your old phone until it actually fails you — upgrade when you are earning, not on Day 2.
  • ✓Take yourself out for a coffee. You are doing something hard. You are allowed to enjoy it.
